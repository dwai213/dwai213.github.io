Comet Surface Sampling Return

Jet Propulsion Laboratory Summer Internship 2013

In the summer between the end of my undergraduate tumble and the start of my graduate career, I went down to sunny, not as smoggy, Pasadena, CA to be a summer intern for the Jet Propulsion Laboratory under the direction of Paul Backes, a group supervisor for Mobility and Manipulation group in the Robotics Section.

One theory posits that comets can potentially have brought the first bits of water and organic compounds to planets like Earth and consequently, gave rise to life. Motivated by this prospect, there is a great need for a comet surface sampling return mission.

I worked with two other interns, Russell Smith and Michael Williamson, to prototype a novel, touch and go architecture, comet sampling tool that Paul designed. We built a prototype to provide proof of concept as well as to hash out some of the finer details of the sampler. In particular, I did the electrical and pneumatics control hardware that actuated the sampler.


  • Picture of the biblade, a set of two blades sheathed in one another. Both blades will take a comet sample, but will leave behind the outermost layer for an efficient, touch and go sampling event.


  • Picture of the rapidly prototyped ball lock, which was inspired by a random McMaster part. This ball lock keeps the two blades together during the sampling event, but will retain one of two blades during the retracting event


  • This is a picture of the biblade sampler prototype with the two air cylinders providing the thrust to push the blades together


  • This is the entire prototype enclosed in a plexigas case to prevent dust and metal from hitting spectators.
